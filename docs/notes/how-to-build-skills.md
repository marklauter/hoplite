A skill is comprehended philosophy reduced to its executable core. `trigger → action` is not the opposite of philosophy; it is philosophy's irreducible, executable form.

## The pipeline

Establish the philosophy, record the kernel, then reduce to trigger→action.

- Establish it in a design interview. Question toward a shared understanding of the kernel.
- Record the kernel: the note, glossary term, or spec that fixes it.
- Reduce it to trigger→action. That reduced form is the skill.

## Comprehend first, then reduce

You cannot reduce what you have not grasped. A `trigger → action` written without the philosophy behind it is a brittle rule-list that breaks on the first case nobody enumerated. Written from comprehended philosophy, the same words are the kernel. They generalize, because a reader can regenerate the philosophy from them. This is comprehension to concision, aimed at skills.

## The worked example is the decompression key

Pair each trigger with a concrete example, a quoted utterance. It lets the next reader re-comprehend the philosophy from the compressed rule, so the compression stays lossless to anyone who engages.

## Two anti-patterns

- The philosophy dump: un-reduced principle, many subsections. It bewilders. This is a concision failure, not a philosophy failure.
- The bare action-list: action with no philosophy behind it. It is brittle and breaks on the novel case.

The skill is neither. It is reduced philosophy, executable, example-calibrated.

## Form

- Lead with the governing verb. A cold reader headlines the first verb they meet, so the description and the opening line both carry it.
- Write in the imperative, spoken to the agent, terse.
- Keep discipline in the skill and mechanics in a companion file; make it stand-alone.
- When interviewing, ask one question at a time with a recommended answer. A wall of questions bewilders, and the user needs that attention for the design.
