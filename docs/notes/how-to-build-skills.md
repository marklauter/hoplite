A skill is comprehended philosophy reduced to its executable core. `trigger → action` is not the opposite of philosophy — it is philosophy's irreducible, executable form.

## The pipeline

Establish the philosophy → record the kernel → reduce to trigger→action.

- **Establish** it in a design interview — question toward a shared understanding of the kernel.
- **Record** the kernel — the note, glossary term, or spec that fixes it.
- **Reduce** it to trigger→action. That reduced form is the skill.

## Comprehend first, then reduce

You cannot reduce what you have not grasped. A `trigger → action` written *without* the philosophy behind it is a brittle rule-list that breaks on the first case nobody enumerated. Written *from* comprehended philosophy, the same words are the kernel — they generalise, because a reader can regenerate the philosophy from them. This is comprehension → concision, aimed at skills.

## The worked example is the decompression key

Pair each trigger with a concrete example — a quoted utterance. It lets the next reader re-comprehend the philosophy *from* the compressed rule, so the compression stays lossless to anyone who engages.

## Two anti-patterns

- **The philosophy dump** — un-reduced principle, many subsections. Bewildering; a concision failure, not a philosophy failure.
- **The bare action-list** — action with no philosophy behind it. Brittle; breaks on the novel case.

The skill is neither: reduced philosophy, executable, example-calibrated.

## Form

- Lead with the governing verb — a cold reader headlines the first verb they meet, so the description and the opening line both carry it.
- Imperative, spoken to the agent, terse.
- Discipline in the skill; mechanics in a companion file; stand-alone.
- When interviewing, ask one question at a time with a recommended answer — a wall of questions is bewildering, and the user needs that attention for the design.
