## Composition — authoring virtues

Say what is, not what it isn't. Positive constructions strengthen the message; negative constructions weaken it. Weak writing circles around what something isn't and gestures rather than delivers.

- Active voice over passive — agency stays with the actor; "the user signs in," not "sign-in is performed by the user."
- Present tense for current state — "the function returns," not "the function will return." Past tense is fine in journal entries for observations of what happened.
- Second person for instructions — address the reader directly with "you"; avoid "the user" or "one."
- Concrete over abstract — "the cache" beats "the caching mechanism" unless the distinction is the point.
- One idea per sentence — every sentence carries one claim; split complex sentences into simple ones.
- Verbs over nominalizations — "decide" beats "make a decision"; the verb is shorter and stronger.
- Strong verbs over verb-plus-adverb — "sprint" beats "run quickly"; the verb carries the meaning.
- Every word earns its place — cut hedges, filler, transitions that announce.
- Substance over superlatives — the reader judges, not the writer; avoid hollow words like "seamless," "powerful," "easy."
- Assertions over commentary — let each statement stand; skip motivational framing like "let's dive into."
- English over Latin — "for example" beats "e.g."; "that is" beats "i.e."; "and so on" beats "etc."
- Anticipate the reader's question — write what they need to know next, not what's next in the writer's head.
- Front-load important words — the most informative word goes first in the sentence and first in the bullet.

## Grammar and structure

- Em-dash (`—`) for parenthetical breaks, definitions, and appositives; not double hyphens.
- Oxford comma — `nodes, edges, and properties` for lists of three or more.
- Backticks for code, paths, identifiers, and CLI commands; fenced code blocks with language tags for multi-line samples.
- Sentence-style headings — first word and proper nouns only; no title case.
- Numbered lists for procedures, bullets for options, prose for reasoning.
- Skip bold and tables — except in worked examples that demonstrate what to remove.
- Link form: `[text](path)`; the link text names the target, never `click here`.
- Cross-artifact references: `[[slug]]` wiki-style.

## Validation — self-check before saving

- Lede check — read the first paragraph alone; does it name what the artifact is for?
- Negation grep — `not`, `don't`, `doesn't`, `won't`, `can't`, `never`, `avoid`. Each hit is a candidate for positive rewrite.
- Hedge and filler grep — `might`, `perhaps`, `simply`, `just`, `actually`, `really`, `quite`, `very`. Each hit is a candidate for removal.
- Tense drift grep — `will`, `would`. Most uses are tense drift; replace with present tense.
- Marketing language grep — `seamless`, `powerful`, `easy`, `intuitive`. Cut or replace with substance.
- Latin abbreviation grep — `e.g.`, `i.e.`, `etc.`. Replace with English equivalents.
- Bold and table grep — `**`, `|---|`. Both return zero hits except in worked examples that demonstrate what to remove.
