# Every word must earn its place

Push for density. Cut hedges, filler, transitions that announce, sentences that restate the previous sentence. Every word removed raises the signal-to-noise ratio of what remains.

Brevity serves clarity.

> "A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts." — Strunk, 1918

## How to apply

- Cut hedges: *might*, *perhaps*, *could be*, *it's worth noting*, *might want to*.
- Cut filler: *basically*, *simply*, *just*, *actually*, *really*, *quite*, *very*, *in order to*.
- Cut transitions that announce instead of saying. "In this section we will discuss X" — say X.
- Replace wordy stock phrases with their short form: *at this point in time* → *now*, *due to the fact that* → *because*, *in the event that* → *if*.
- Write assertions, not commentary. Skip motivational framing and appeals to virtue; let each statement stand.
- Stop cutting when the shorter version asks the reader to reconstruct what the longer version said outright — that is the cut-line.

## Examples

### Scaffold redundancy

- Weak: The reason he left his job at the bank was because his health began to fail.
- Strong: He left his job at the bank because his health began to fail.

"The reason... was because" is scaffolding around the word that already carries the relationship. Cut the scaffold, keep `because`.

### Aggregate cuts

- Weak: While it is crucial for us to speak out on behalf of education, it is important that we do so in a manner consistent with statute and administrative rules.
- Strong: We need to speak out on behalf of education while observing statute and administrative rules.

Three cuts compound: "it is crucial for us to" → drop; "it is important that we do so" → drop; "in a manner consistent with" → "observing."
